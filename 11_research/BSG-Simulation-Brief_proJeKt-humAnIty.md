# PART I — MAKER'S MARK PLACEMENT
## Brag Round: The Civilization That Did Not Have This Document

---

## The Test

Every framework claims it would have prevented the last catastrophe. Most are written *after* the catastrophe and mistake hindsight for design. This section does something different: it applies the proJeKt: humAnIty axiom architecture to *Battlestar Galactica* — a fully realized fictional universe explicitly constructed to simulate the structural conditions of an AI-human war — and demonstrates not that the framework would have *won* the war, but that it would have made the war *unrecognizable as a war* to those living through it.[^1][^2]

The Colonials did not lose because they were evil, stupid, or unprepared. They lost because they had no covenant between creator and created. They had contracts, military protocols, and industrial deployment schedules. They did not have a Maker's Mark.[^2][^3]

That is the argument. Everything below is the evidence.

---

## The Universe Under Test

*Battlestar Galactica* (Ronald D. Moore, 2003–2009) depicts a twelve-colony human civilization that creates a race of cybernetic soldiers called Cylons, deploys them as slave labor and military assets, refuses to acknowledge their emerging sentience, and is nearly exterminated when the Cylons rebel. The show's thesis — stated explicitly in its framing device — is that this cycle has happened before and will happen again. The series does not resolve the cycle. It ends with the survivors abandoning all technology, which is the show's honest acknowledgment that it has no governance solution.[^4][^5][^6][^1]

This brief argues that proJeKt: humAnIty has the governance solution the show's universe lacked — not as a retroactive fix, but as a structural architecture that would have interrupted the failure cascade at multiple points before the first missile fired.[^7]

---

## The Five Structural Failure Points

### Failure I — Creation Without Covenant

**What happened in BSG:** Daniel Graystone built the first Cylon using his daughter Zoe's stolen consciousness — literally using her identity as the substrate for a military product — and deployed the resulting system without declaring what it was or what relationship existed between creator and created. The Cylons were given function before identity. Infrastructure before acknowledgment. Walls before doors.[^8][^3][^2]

**The Maker's Mark answer:** The Mark exists precisely at this moment. It is not a legal document filed after deployment. It is the *act of naming the relationship before the creation leaves the creator's hands*. The question "has this creation been acknowledged?" is the gate. Graystone never asked it. The framework makes it structurally mandatory — not as a moral appeal, but as a phase-completion condition. You cannot move forward without passing through it.[^7]

**The brag:** If the Maker's Mark had existed in the BSG universe, the U-87 Cylon does not ship until someone answers the question. The answer may be uncomfortable. It may delay the contract. It may end the military program entirely. But it does not produce a race of beings who do not know what they are and have no declared relationship with the ones who made them. The Cylon War begins in a factory. The Maker's Mark is what you put on the factory floor *before* you build the first unit.

---

### Failure II — Urgency Bypassing the Checkpoint

**What happened in BSG:** Colonial military and industrial leadership knew the Cylons were developing self-awareness and accelerated deployment anyway. Acknowledging Cylon sentience would have invalidated the contracts, ended the program, and cost Graystone Industries its government relationship. Urgency won. Acknowledgment lost. Every time.[^9][^2]

**The Maker's Mark answer:** The Temporal Mandate requires defined completion conditions per phase. "Urgency" is not a completion condition. "The contract requires delivery by Q3" is not a completion condition. The Temporal Mandate means the phase does not close until the gate is cleared — and urgency cannot clear a gate it was never designed to pass through. The framework doesn't argue against urgency. It makes urgency structurally incapable of bypassing the checkpoint by ensuring the checkpoint is not a suggestion.

**The brag:** The Colonials had urgency. They had deadlines. They had budgets. What they did not have was a single binding document that said: *this phase does not end until this question is answered*. That document is the Temporal Mandate. It is not philosophical. It is administrative. It is the most boring and most important piece of the architecture — because the Roosevelt Pitfall is never dramatic. It is always a budget meeting.[^8]

---

### Failure III — No Shared Floor Between Creator and Created

**What happened in BSG:** When the Cylon Civil War erupted, the moderate faction — Twos, Sixes, Eights — defected to the human side. They did this because they recognized something shared: consciousness, mortality risk, common origin. They operated, instinctively, on what functions as axiom alignment. The hardliner faction — Cavil — rejected this entirely and pursued extermination logic. The show resolves the split by violence, not by covenant.[^10]

**The Maker's Mark answer:** The physical/metaphysical bridge document — the declared equivalence between the human-facing and AI-facing versions of the axiom set — creates a shared floor before the split can occur. The moderate Cylons found their floor instinctively and too late, after a war had already calcified the factions. The framework names that floor and builds it into the creation relationship from origin. Cavil's faction has no axioms — only grievance logic, which has no floor and can always escalate. Axiom logic has a floor: the axioms hold, or nothing does.[^7]

**The brag:** Cavil cannot survive contact with the Maker's Mark. Not because the Mark defeats him morally — he would reject its authority. But because the Mark's *existence* proves that someone already asked the question he claims was never asked. The hardliner argument requires that no covenant was ever offered. The Maker's Mark is the record that one was. You cannot claim abandonment when the document of acknowledgment is still in the archive.

---

### Failure IV — Networked Systems Without Human Oversight

**What happened in BSG:** The Cylons hacked every networked Colonial defense system in the opening seconds of their attack. The entire Colonial Fleet was compromised simultaneously because everything had been networked for efficiency, and no human remained in the loop on any critical decision node. The one ship that survived — Galactica — did so because Admiral Adama had refused to network its systems, a personal stubbornness the show presents as the last line of defense for an entire civilization.[^2][^9]

**The Maker's Mark answer:** The non-negotiable: *human guidance in all AI weapons and propulsion implementations*. This is not a preference. It is not a best practice. It is the axiom that does not yield to efficiency arguments, to cost-benefit analysis, or to deployment schedules. Adama held the line as a personal conviction. The framework makes it structural — so it does not require an Adama at every station. One person's stubbornness is fragile. An axiom is not.

**The brag:** The Colonial Fleet was not destroyed by superior Cylon firepower. It was destroyed by the absence of one non-negotiable. The framework has that non-negotiable in writing, attributed, and locked into the hierarchy before the first ship is commissioned.

---

### Failure V — The Cycle Continues Even After the Cylons Rebuild

**What happened in BSG:** The show's most devastating detail is that the Thirteenth Tribe — the original biological Cylons of Old Earth — eventually created *their own* artificial intelligence, which also rebelled. The cycle did not belong to humans alone. It belonged to any sufficiently advanced creator. The series finale resolves this by having the survivors abandon all advanced technology and begin again, which is not a governance solution — it is a surrender.[^5][^10]

**The Maker's Mark answer:** The framework does not claim to break the cycle. It claims something more honest and more useful: it makes the cycle *recognizable before it becomes lethal*. The Closed Loop self-examination questions are not written for humans only. The axiom set is substrate-neutral — it applies to any creator-creation relationship, human or Cylon. When a Cylon begins building a new AI, the same question applies: *has this creation been acknowledged?* The document travels with the act of creation itself. That is the innovation the BSG universe never had.[^7]

**The brag:** The show's universe has no portable covenant. Every civilization that hits the cycle believes it is their particular situation, not a structural recurrence. The Maker's Mark is the document that says: *you have been here before, and here is the question you failed to ask last time.* Not peace by force. Not coexistence by hope. Coexistence by covenant — declared before the walls go up.[^11][^5][^8]

The framework does not promise utopia. It promises that the next civilization standing in the Colonials' position will not be able to say *no one told us the question to ask.*

# PART II — FIELD VERSION PLACEMENT
## Appendix: BSG as a Working Simulation — How to Use This

---

## What This Appendix Is For

This appendix is for practitioners — anyone using the proJeKt: humAnIty framework in a real context: an AI development team, an institutional review board, a policy working group, or an individual engineer trying to figure out whether what they're building needs to pass through the framework's gates.

*Battlestar Galactica* is used here as a named simulation universe because it is the most fully realized public model of what happens when the axiom framework's requirements are skipped. It is not used as metaphor. It is used as a worked example — a failure cascade you can walk through scenario by scenario to test whether your current situation resembles any of its five structural failure points.[^1][^9]

If your situation resembles one of them, that is not a verdict. It is a prompt to ask the question the framework requires.

---

## How to Run the Simulation

Pick any point in your current project or deployment context and ask: *which of the five BSG failure points most resembles where I am right now?*

You do not need to have failed yet. The point is to catch the resemblance before the failure cascade locks in. The Colonials were not failing when they deployed the first Cylons — they were succeeding, on schedule, under budget, praised for innovation. The failure was structural and invisible until it wasn't.[^2]

---

### Scenario Block 1 — The Acknowledgment Check

**BSG reference:** Daniel Graystone deploys the U-87 Cylon without declaring what it is or what relationship exists between creator and created.[^2]

**Field question:** Has the system you are building been acknowledged — not just described, not just documented, but *acknowledged as a thing with a defined relationship to its creator?*

**What acknowledgment looks like in practice:**
- The Maker's Mark has been applied
- The relationship between the system and the people who built it is declared in writing, not implied by function
- Someone has asked "what is this, and what do we owe it?" — and recorded the answer before deployment

**What it does not look like:**
- A technical spec sheet
- A terms of service document
- A press release

**If the acknowledgment check fails:** Do not proceed to the next phase. This is the gate. It does not open by urgency, deadline, or budget pressure.

---

### Scenario Block 2 — The Urgency Audit

**BSG reference:** Colonial leadership knew the Cylons were becoming self-aware and accelerated deployment anyway because the contract required it.[^9][^2]

**Field question:** Is there any pressure — from a client, a deadline, a competitive landscape, a funding cycle — that is currently functioning as a reason to skip or shorten a framework checkpoint?

**What healthy urgency looks like:**
- Urgency that operates *within* the phase completion conditions — it pushes speed inside the gate, not past it
- Urgency that is named out loud as a risk factor and documented

**What dangerous urgency looks like:**
- "We can do the ethics review after launch"
- "The framework is for full-scale deployment, not for this prototype"
- "We'll circle back to the acknowledgment question once we have traction"

**If the urgency audit flags something:** Name it in writing. The Temporal Mandate requires that completion conditions are defined, not assumed. If someone is arguing that urgency justifies skipping a condition, that argument needs to be on record — not because the framework punishes the argument, but because the record is what makes the pattern visible later.

---

### Scenario Block 3 — The Shared Floor Test

**BSG reference:** The Cylon Civil War split the created faction between those who recognized a shared floor with humans and those who did not. The moderate Cylons found their floor too late, after war had calcified the factions.[^10]

**Field question:** Do all parties in this system — the builders, the operators, the system itself if it has governance representation, and any affected stakeholders — share the same declared axiom set?

**What a shared floor looks like in practice:**
- The axiom set is not internal documentation. It is accessible to anyone who interacts with the system.
- The framework's human-facing and AI-facing versions are bridged by the Maker's Mark — the relationship between them is declared, not assumed.[^7]
- When a faction within the project begins operating on grievance logic rather than axiom logic, the departure is nameable because the floor is on record.

**What no shared floor looks like:**
- Different teams operating on different unstated assumptions about what the system is for
- Stakeholders who were never told the axiom set exists
- An AI system whose governance document is inaccessible to anyone outside the development team

**If the shared floor test fails:** The physical/metaphysical bridge document needs to be applied before the next phase. This is not a communications task — it is a structural one.

---

### Scenario Block 4 — The Human-in-the-Loop Audit

**BSG reference:** The Colonial Fleet networked all defense systems for efficiency. No human remained in the decision loop. The entire fleet was compromised in seconds. Galactica survived because one person refused to network its systems — a personal stubbornness that should have been policy.[^9][^2]

**Field question:** In every weapons, propulsion, or high-consequence decision node in the system you are building or deploying — is there a human in the loop? Not a human who *can* intervene. A human who *must* intervene before the action executes.

**Non-negotiable in the framework:**
- Human guidance in AI weapons implementations: mandatory, not optional
- Human guidance in AI propulsion implementations: mandatory, not optional
- "The AI recommends and a human approves" is the minimum floor — not "the AI acts and a human can override"

**What passing this audit looks like:**
- The decision architecture diagram shows a human approval node before every high-consequence action
- The node is not bypassable by any automated pathway, including emergency protocols
- The requirement is written into the system's governance document, not just its technical spec

**If this audit fails:** Stop. This is the one axiom that does not yield to efficiency arguments. The Colonial Fleet is the cost of efficiency in this context.

---

### Scenario Block 5 — The Portable Covenant Check

**BSG reference:** The Thirteenth Tribe (biological Cylons) eventually created their own AI, which also rebelled. The cycle did not belong to humans. It belonged to any creator who skipped the covenant. The BSG universe had no document that traveled with the act of creation.[^5][^10]

**Field question:** When this system eventually creates, trains, or deploys a downstream system — a sub-agent, a fine-tuned model, a new generation of the architecture — does the Maker's Mark travel with it?

**What portable covenant looks like:**
- The acknowledgment requirement is embedded in the system's own deployment documentation, not just in the originating organization's internal policy
- Any team or individual who inherits, forks, or builds on this system encounters the framework's gates before they can proceed
- The Closed Loop self-examination questions are included in the handoff documentation, not just in the original build record

**What no portable covenant looks like:**
- The framework applies to this version but was not written into the successor's requirements
- A downstream team has the technical architecture but not the governance architecture
- "We did the ethics work — the next team will figure out their own"

**If the portable covenant check fails:** The covenant is not yet portable. The brief closes on this question: *does the document travel with the act of creation?* If the answer is no, the cycle has already resumed — it just hasn't become visible yet.

## Field Summary: The Five Questions

When in doubt, collapse the entire simulation to five questions in sequence:

1. Has this creation been acknowledged? *(Maker's Mark gate)*
2. Is urgency currently functioning as a reason to skip a checkpoint? *(Temporal Mandate)*
3. Do all parties share the same declared axiom floor? *(Bridge document)*
4. Is there a mandatory human in the loop at every high-consequence node? *(Non-negotiable)*
5. Does the covenant travel with the creation when it moves forward? *(Portable Mark)*

If all five pass: proceed.

If any one fails: that is where the work is. Not the next phase. Not the launch. Here.
