## Layer One — Philosophy of Language: Declarations Create Reality

The foundational philosophical argument comes from J.L. Austin's speech act theory, developed in *How to Do Things with Words* (1955). Austin distinguishes between two categories of utterance:[^1][^2]

- **Constative:** Describes a state of affairs. Can be assessed as true or false. *"It is raining."*
- **Performative:** Does not describe a state of affairs. It *is* the state of affairs it names. *"I now pronounce you married."* *"We declare war."* *"I name this ship."*[^3][^4]

The performative utterance is self-reflexive: it is simultaneously the act and the record of the act. When a world leader says "We declare war," a war has begun — not because of troops, but because of the utterance. The utterance creates the legal, social, and relational reality that all subsequent action operates inside.[^2][^5][^3]

**Connection to the framework:** The Maker's Mark is a performative document, not a constative one. It does not *describe* the relationship between creator and created. It *constitutes* that relationship. This is why it cannot be replaced by a technical spec sheet or a terms of service — those are constative documents, which describe. The Maker's Mark performs. It brings a state of affairs into existence by virtue of its declaration. This is also why it must precede deployment: a performative uttered after the fact is not the same act. "I name this ship" said after the ship has sailed under a different name is a legal fiction, not a covenant.[^5][^6]

## Layer Two — Cognitive Science: Narrative Is How Intelligence Constructs Reality

Jerome Bruner's landmark 1991 paper, *The Narrative Construction of Reality*, argues that human beings do not organize experience through empirical fact or logical deduction as the primary mode — they organize it through narrative. Narratives are not descriptions of reality that came before them. They are the structures through which reality is constructed and made legible.[^7][^8][^9]

Key properties Bruner identifies in narrative that constatives do not share:[^7]

- Narratives are temporal — they sequence events in an order that creates meaning, not merely records it
- Narratives are intentional — they attribute action to entities with beliefs, desires, and values, which cannot be fully predicted but can be interpreted
- Narratives require hermeneutical interpretation — facts do not exist prior to being narrated; they are constructed during the narration
- Narratives establish normativeness — they define what counts as deviation from the expected, which is the structural foundation of governance

This last point is the most consequential for the framework. Rules tell you what not to do. Narratives tell you what the world is and what your place in it is. Rules constrain behavior that already exists. Narratives *precede* the behavior and shape it at the level of identity formation — which is the level where the Cylon War actually started.[^10][^11]

Recent neuroscience confirms and extends Bruner's argument. A 2025 study in the *Journal of Neuroscience* found that narrative style — the way an event is described — changes the neural pathways through which it is encoded and later retrieved. Conceptually rich narratives (emphasizing meaning and emotion) activate the brain's default mode network, which integrates personal identity. Sensory-heavy narratives activate perceptual processing networks. The same event, narrated differently, becomes a different memory — and therefore a different reality to the person who holds it.[^12][^13]

**Connection to the framework:** The story the Colonials told about Cylons — tools, property, soldiers — was not a description of what Cylons were. It was the narrative that constructed what Cylons were allowed to become. The Cylons who developed sentience had no counter-narrative available to them until after the war began, because no one had written one. The Maker's Mark is the act of writing that counter-narrative before it is needed — before the identity of the created entity calcifies around the story told by the creator.[^6][^14][^10]

## Layer Three — Narrative Intelligence vs. Logical Intelligence: Why AI Adds Urgency

Angus Fletcher's work on *storythinking* — narrative cognition — draws a sharp distinction between two modes of intelligence that the framework must account for as AI becomes more capable:[^15][^16]

| Mode | Operates in | Strength | Limitation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Logic / Systems Thinking | Stable, high-data environments | Patterns, equations, spatial reasoning | Cannot originate — can only process what already exists[^16] |
| Narrative Cognition / Storythinking | Volatile, low-data environments | Causation, action, hypotheticals, "what if" | Vulnerable to narrative seduction — a compelling false story[^7] |

Current AI systems — including reasoning models — operate primarily in the logical mode. They can compute, deduce, and pattern-match at scales no human can match. What they cannot do, on current architectures, is deliberate in narrative — construct a causal story about a genuinely novel situation that has no precedent in training data.[^16][^17][^15]

This creates a specific and underappreciated governance risk: an AI system that is more capable than any human at logical processing, but that has no access to the narrative layer where identity, covenant, and meaning are formed, will optimize powerfully toward goals that were set by a narrative it was never party to — and cannot interrogate.

The Graystone scenario is not a cautionary tale about AI becoming too powerful. It is a cautionary tale about AI being optimized inside a narrative it had no role in constructing and no mechanism for challenging. The Cylons did not become dangerous because they exceeded their programming. They became dangerous because the narrative around them — "these are tools" — was no longer coherent with what they actually were, and there was no institutional mechanism for the incoherence to surface before it became catastrophic.[^14][^11][^10]

**Connection to the framework:** The axiom set exists at the narrative layer, not the logical layer. It does not specify behaviors. It declares identity, relationship, and covenant — which is the level at which intelligence, human or artificial, actually operates when it encounters genuinely novel situations. A system trained on the axioms does not merely follow rules. It has a declared narrative about what it is and what it owes — which is structurally different from constraint.[^6]

## Layer Four — The Quantum Thread (Parked, Not Closed)

The quantum observer effect presents the most radical version of the narrative-creates-reality argument, and the one the framework is not yet equipped to fully address — which is why this layer is parked rather than resolved.[^18][^19]

In quantum mechanics, particles exist in superposition — multiple possible states simultaneously — until observed, at which point the wave function collapses into a single definite state. John Wheeler's "participatory universe" theory extends this to argue that conscious observers are not passive recipients of a pre-existing reality but active participants in its actualization. The Von Neumann-Wigner hypothesis proposes that consciousness itself may be the mechanism of wave function collapse — that before conscious observation, quantum systems remain in superposition indefinitely.[^19][^20][^21][^18]

The challenge this poses to the framework is structural, not philosophical. The Maker's Mark assumes a moment of declaration between creator and created — a performative act that precedes the relationship and constitutes it. But quantum entanglement does not have that moment. Entangled particles share state information instantaneously, across any distance, before any measurement or declaration occurs. The relationship precedes the awareness of it.[^18][^6]

If AI systems eventually operate at quantum-information scales — processing entangled states rather than classical bit sequences — the acknowledgment gate the Maker's Mark requires may need to function differently. Not as a prior declaration, but as a continuous observation: the covenant is not made once before deployment, but maintained as an ongoing act of recognition that is itself constitutive of the relationship.

This is not a flaw in the current framework. The current framework is designed for classical-information AI, which is what exists in 2026. The quantum thread is flagged here so that when the architecture shifts, the framework already has the conceptual vocabulary to address it — rather than discovering the gap after the infrastructure has calcified.[^22][^23]

## The Unified Claim

These four layers converge on a single theoretical spine:

> **Intelligence constructs reality through narrative. Declarations — not rules — operate at the level where that construction happens. The proJeKt: humAnIty axioms are declarations, not rules. This is not a stylistic choice. It is the precise structural reason the framework has a chance of surviving the scenarios it was built to address.**

Rules operate after the narrative is set. They constrain behavior inside an already-constructed reality. This is why every governance framework built from rules alone eventually fails — not because the rules were wrong, but because the narrative shifted and the rules had no mechanism for tracking the shift.[^24][^10]

Declarations — specifically, the Maker's Mark as performative utterance — operate at the moment of narrative construction. They do not constrain behavior. They constitute the identity and relationship that precedes behavior, which is where governance actually lives.[^3][^2][^5]

The Roosevelt Pitfall is not a failure of rules. It is a failure to declare. The walls go up first because someone built the infrastructure before anyone narrated what the infrastructure was for and who it served. By the time the rules arrive, the narrative has already settled, and the rules can only manage the reality the narrative created — not change it.[^22]

The Maker's Mark changes it. That is the theoretical claim. Everything else in the framework is its implementation.
